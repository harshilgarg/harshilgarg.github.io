Balancing government intelligence and civilian privacy rights in the digital age.
Harshil Garg & Andrew Lin
Duke University

PRISM is a clandestine operation funded, developed, and maintained by the National Security Agency (NSA), which is exclusively designed to monitor private communication and electronic activities in the United States, and even on an international scale to some degree. PRISM is the latest evolution of the US government's extensive efforts to counter terrorism after 9/11 which initiated with the Patriot Act under President Bush and has since expanded to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA). Since its creation in 2007, experts claim that PRISM has exploited provisions in the United States penal code to pressure companies with heavy Internet traffic, like Google, Yahoo, Facebook, and Microsoft, into providing private user data. With this server access, the NSA has a direct pipeline to user search history, email content, file transfers, live chats, and records of many other personal and private Internet activities.

Under the PRISM program, NSA monitors content including emails, messages, browser history and personal files.
— The Guardian
PRISM: A formidable surveillance weapon.

PRISM's operations were highly secretive until exposed by a former Booz Allen Hamilton security contractor, Edward Snowden. He released slides detailing how nine companies are required to fork over user data to the NSA. However, in the aftermath, the implicated companies vehemently denied knowledge of and participation in PRISM and rejected allegations that the US government is able to directly tap into users' data.

How powerful is PRISM? Extremely.

PRISM provides the FBI and NSA access to virtually all forms of online communication. According to the provisions of the legislations that indirectly permit the existence of PRISM, only non-American citizens outside of the United States can be targeted. However, incidental data from American citizens is still collected inadvertently by this program, but supposedly minimized. Most troubling, however, is the disregard for the basic privacy rights that are explicitly enumerated in the United States Constitution.

By using underseas cable taps, data hubs, and satellite signal receptors peppered throughout the United States, the NSA can intercept civilian communications as shown below. With this advanced technology, it is clear that collecting private American citizen data is unavoidable.

It is clear the NSA is highly unregulated by the federal court system and operates autonomously, collecting even "incidental" data unavoidably due to the nature of the network involved.
